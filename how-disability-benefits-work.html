How Disability Benefits Work (2026 Guide)

Last updated: April 11, 2026 — Figures verified against SSA.gov, VA.gov, and official state program sources | Reviewed by the Disability Trust AI editorial team

There is no single "disability benefit" in the United States. There are at least four separate systems, run by different agencies, funded by different sources, with different rules for who gets in and how much they receive. Most people don't learn this until they've already filed for the wrong one.

That confusion costs real money. It costs months — sometimes years — of processing time on claims that were never going to be approved under the program they were filed with. It means missing out on benefits a person was perfectly eligible for, simply because no one explained that a second or third program existed.

This page breaks the entire system apart. Not in vague, government-brochure language, but in the blunt terms people actually need before they file anything. You'll understand how each program works, who it's designed for, how the government decides who gets approved, how money moves, and — most critically — which combination of programs applies to your situation.

Read this page first. Then go deeper into the specific program guides once you know where you belong.

Start Here: What You Need to Know

  • Disability benefits are not one program. There are four major categories, and they don't all come from the same place.
  • Eligibility is different for each one. You may qualify for one, two, or even three programs at once — or none.
  • The program you file under changes everything — how long you wait, what you get paid, and what health insurance comes with it.
  • Filing for the wrong program is the #1 non-medical reason for denial.

What Counts as a Disability Benefit?

A disability benefit is a recurring government payment to someone whose physical or mental condition prevents them from supporting themselves through work — or, in the case of veteran benefits, compensates them for health damage connected to military service.

That definition sounds simple. The system behind it is not. In the U.S., "disability benefits" can refer to any of the following:

Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) is a federal program administered by the Social Security Administration. It pays workers who've contributed payroll taxes long enough to earn coverage, then become too disabled to work. It's insurance — you paid in, and now you're collecting on that policy. Our complete SSDI guide covers this program in detail.

Supplemental Security Income (SSI) is also federal and also run by the SSA, but it's an entirely different animal. SSI doesn't require any work history. It's a needs-based safety net for people with disabilities (or who are 65+) who have extremely limited income and assets. It's funded from general tax revenue, not payroll deductions. See our complete SSI guide for the full breakdown.

VA Disability Compensation is paid by the Department of Veterans Affairs to veterans whose injuries or illnesses are connected to their military service. The VA system operates on a completely different logic: you don't have to prove you can't work. You have to prove your condition is service-connected. Ratings run from 0% to 100%, and compensation scales accordingly. Our VA disability benefits guide walks through the entire claims process.

State disability programs exist in only a handful of states. These are typically short-term programs — Temporary Disability Insurance (TDI) — that replace a portion of wages while someone recovers from a non-work-related injury or illness. Only California, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, Hawaii, and Puerto Rico currently operate TDI programs. Some states also add supplemental payments on top of federal SSI. Our state disability programs guide covers what's available and where.

Short-Term vs. Long-Term: A Critical Distinction

State TDI programs are designed for temporary conditions — recovery from surgery, a broken bone, a pregnancy complication. They typically last 26 to 52 weeks. SSDI and SSI, by contrast, require conditions expected to last at least 12 months or result in death. VA disability compensation can be temporary or permanent, depending on the rating decision.

Confusing a short-term program with a long-term one (or not knowing the short-term option exists) is one of the most common mistakes people make. Someone recovering from back surgery in California who files for SSDI instead of State Disability Insurance may wait 6+ months for a decision on a claim that didn't need to be filed federally at all.

The 4 Main Types of Disability Benefits

Every disability benefit in the country falls into one of four categories. Understanding these categories is more useful than memorizing program names, because the category tells you why a program exists and who it was built for.

Category Program Who It's For Funding Source Key Requirement

Federal Needs-Based SSI Disabled or elderly individuals with very limited income and assets, regardless of work history General tax revenue Below income/asset limits + medical disability (or age 65+)

State-Administered TDI / SDI (varies by state) Workers in participating states with temporary, non-work-related disabilities State payroll taxes (employee and/or employer) Recent employment in a TDI state + temporary medical condition

Federal Work-Based (SSDI)

SSDI functions like a disability insurance policy that working Americans automatically pay into through every paycheck. The Social Security Administration tracks your contributions as "work credits." You earn up to four credits per year, and most adults need 40 credits total (roughly 10 years of work) with 20 earned in the last decade to qualify. Younger workers need fewer. The payout is based on your lifetime earnings, not a flat rate — higher earners who become disabled receive higher monthly payments.

Federal Needs-Based (SSI)

SSI exists for people who never worked enough to earn SSDI coverage — or who worked but earned very little. It's also available to disabled children and adults 65 and older. The trade-off: eligibility requires proving that your countable resources fall below $2,000 for an individual ($3,000 for a couple), and your income is low enough to remain below program thresholds. The monthly payment is a flat federal rate — $967 per individual in 2026 — with some states adding a supplement.

Military / Veteran (VA Disability)

VA disability compensation is the only major disability benefit that doesn't require you to be unable to work. A veteran with a 70% disability rating can hold a full-time job and still collect monthly compensation. Eligibility depends entirely on establishing a "service connection" — proof that a current medical condition was caused by, started during, or was made worse by military service. Compensation scales with the severity rating, from $175/month at 10% to over $3,800/month at 100% before dependent additions.

State-Administered (TDI / SDI)

Only six jurisdictions — California, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, Hawaii, and Puerto Rico — require employers to provide temporary disability insurance. These programs replace a percentage of wages (typically 50–70%) for workers with short-term medical conditions that prevent them from doing their job. Coverage usually lasts 26 to 52 weeks. Workers in the other 44 states have no state-level disability safety net unless their employer voluntarily offers short-term disability insurance.

How Eligibility Generally Works

Every disability program has its own gatekeeping criteria. But the general logic breaks into five categories of proof. Not every program requires all five — but if you understand what each one means, you can quickly assess where you stand before investing months in an application.

1. Medical Proof

Every program requires medical documentation. The standard varies. SSDI and SSI require evidence that your condition prevents you from performing "substantial gainful activity" and will last at least 12 months or result in death. The VA requires a current diagnosis plus evidence linking it to service. State TDI programs require a physician's certification that you cannot do your current job, but the bar is lower — the condition doesn't have to be permanent or even long-term.

In all cases, the strength and specificity of your medical records matters more than the diagnosis itself. A well-documented moderate condition with clear functional limitations often fares better than a severe diagnosis with thin records.

2. Work History

SSDI requires sufficient work credits earned through payroll-tax-covered employment. State TDI programs require recent covered employment in that specific state. SSI and VA disability have no work history requirement at all — that's part of what makes them accessible to different populations.

3. Income and Assets

SSI has strict limits: $2,000 in countable assets for individuals, and income thresholds that reduce your payment dollar-for-dollar in many cases. SSDI doesn't test your assets, but it does cap how much you can earn while collecting — the "substantial gainful activity" limit is $1,620/month in 2026 for non-blind individuals. VA disability has no income or asset test whatsoever. State TDI programs generally require that you're not working (or working reduced hours) while claiming benefits.

4. Service Connection (VA Only)

This is unique to VA disability. You must establish that your condition is directly related to military service — either caused by it, aggravated during it, or secondary to another service-connected condition. Presumptive conditions (like certain cancers linked to burn pit exposure under the PACT Act) streamline this requirement for specific circumstances.

5. State-Specific Rules

If you're applying for a state program, every detail depends on which state you live in. California's SDI covers up to 70% of wages with a high ceiling. New York's program caps at $170 per week. Some states offer SSI supplements automatically; others require a separate application. There is no national standard — each state's rules must be checked individually.

Don't Assume You Know Your Eligibility

The single most expensive mistake in the disability system is self-screening out of a program you actually qualify for — or filing for one where you clearly don't meet the threshold. Before you apply for anything, check the specific eligibility criteria for every program that could possibly apply to you. Our individual program guides (SSDI, SSI, VA, state programs) break these down in full.

How the Government Decides

Filing an application is just the starting line. What happens next depends on which agency is reviewing your claim and what criteria they use to judge it.

Medical Qualification

For SSDI and SSI, the Social Security Administration uses a sequential evaluation process. Your claim goes to a state-level agency called Disability Determination Services (DDS), where a team of medical and vocational professionals reviews your records. They assess whether your condition matches or equals a listing in the SSA's "Blue Book" of qualifying impairments. If it doesn't match a listing directly, they evaluate whether your remaining functional capacity still allows you to do any work that exists in the national economy.

For VA disability, the process centers on a Compensation and Pension (C&P) exam. A VA-contracted examiner evaluates your condition, confirms the diagnosis, and — critically — provides an opinion on whether it's connected to your military service. That examiner's report carries enormous weight in the rating decision.

State TDI programs typically require certification from your treating physician, and the state agency verifies that your employment records meet the coverage requirements. The medical bar is lower: you just need to show you can't perform your current job duties temporarily.

Non-Medical Qualification

Even if you're medically qualified, non-medical factors can sink a claim. SSDI requires enough work credits. SSI requires financial eligibility — your assets and income are verified, and exceeding the limits at any point during the process is disqualifying. VA claims require proof of veteran status and service connection. State TDI requires covered employment in the right state within a qualifying base period.

Timelines

Initial SSDI and SSI decisions typically take 3 to 7 months. If denied and you appeal, reconsideration adds another 3 to 5 months. A hearing before an administrative law judge — where approval rates are highest — can take 12 to 18 months from the initial denial. Total time from application to hearing-level approval frequently exceeds two years.

VA disability claims average 125 to 150 days for an initial decision, though complex claims or appeals can stretch much longer. State TDI decisions are the fastest: most states process claims in 2 to 4 weeks.

Approvals vs. Denials

Initial approval rates for SSDI and SSI hover around 30–40%, depending on the state and the condition. That number is deceptive — many initial denials are overturned on appeal. At the hearing level, approval rates climb to 45–55% nationally. The overall chance of eventual approval, for those who persist through the full appeals process, is meaningfully higher than the initial approval rate suggests.

VA disability initial approval rates are generally higher, particularly for conditions with presumptive service connection. State TDI approval rates are the highest of all four categories, since the medical and employment requirements are more straightforward.

How Payments Work

The amount you receive, when you start receiving it, and what additional coverage comes with it varies dramatically across programs.

Monthly Payments

SSDI payments are personalized. They're calculated from your average indexed monthly earnings over your working life. Someone who earned $60,000/year for 20 years will receive a different amount than someone who earned $30,000 for 10 years. The national average is approximately $1,630/month in 2026, but individual amounts range widely.

SSI payments are standardized. The federal benefit rate is $967/month for an eligible individual. That amount decreases if you have other income, and it can increase if your state adds a supplement. Your living situation matters too — living in someone else's household and not paying your share of expenses can reduce your SSI payment.

VA disability compensation follows a fixed rate schedule tied to your disability rating. A veteran rated at 50% receives $1,110/month. At 100%, the base rate is $3,832/month, with additional payments for a spouse, children, or dependent parents. The VA also awards Special Monthly Compensation for specific severe conditions.

Back Pay

SSDI can pay retroactive benefits up to 12 months before your application date (after the five-month waiting period). If you became disabled 18 months before applying, you could receive a lump sum covering 12 months of back benefits.

SSI pays only from the date of application — there's no retroactive period. This is one reason filing promptly matters for SSI claimants.

VA back pay is calculated from the "effective date" the VA assigns, which is often the date of the original claim. For claims that take months or years to process, back pay can be substantial.

Health Coverage Attached to Benefits

SSDI recipients become eligible for Medicare 24 months after their first disability payment. That means a total wait of 29 months from disability onset (5-month waiting period + 24 months). This is a significant gap for people without other health insurance.

SSI recipients typically qualify for Medicaid immediately upon approval. In most states, SSI approval triggers automatic Medicaid enrollment. This is one of SSI's most valuable features — immediate health coverage for the population least able to afford it.

Veterans with VA disability ratings can enroll in VA healthcare, which is a separate benefit from disability compensation. Higher-rated veterans get priority enrollment and reduced or eliminated copays.

Which Program Fits Which Person?

Theory doesn't help much when you're staring at application forms. Here's how real-life situations map to specific programs.

Find Your Situation

Identify the scenario below that's closest to yours, then follow the guidance for which program to pursue. Many people match more than one — that's normal, and often means you should apply for multiple programs.

1

You worked steadily for years and now can't work due to a medical condition

Primary program: SSDI. If you have 10+ years of work history with payroll taxes, SSDI is your main path. Your payment will reflect your earnings. You don't need to be broke — SSDI doesn't test assets. If your savings are low, you may also qualify for SSI concurrently. Read the SSDI guide and the SSDI vs. SSI comparison.

2

You have little or no work history, limited income, and a disabling condition

Primary program: SSI. SSI doesn't require work credits. It's designed for exactly this situation. Expect strict income and asset scrutiny, but no employment prerequisites. Approval also gives you immediate Medicaid in most states. See the SSI guide.

3

You're a veteran with health issues connected to your military service

Primary program: VA disability compensation. This applies whether or not you can still work. If your condition started during service, was aggravated during service, or is secondary to a service-connected condition, the VA process is your lane. If you also can't work and have a civilian work history, SSDI may apply separately. Explore the VA disability guide.

4

You live in a TDI state and have a temporary condition

Primary program: State TDI / SDI. If you're employed in California, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, Hawaii, or Puerto Rico and you have a short-term medical condition, start with your state's temporary disability program. It pays faster and covers exactly this scenario. Only pursue SSDI or SSI if your condition lasts beyond 12 months. Details in our state programs guide.

5

You might qualify for more than one program

Action: Apply for all that fit. A veteran with a service-connected condition AND a civilian work history can collect both VA compensation and SSDI — they don't reduce each other. A worker with low SSDI payments may qualify for supplemental SSI. Filing for one program does not disqualify you from another. The section below explains how concurrent benefits work.

Can You Get More Than One Benefit?

Yes — but how they interact depends on the specific combination. Some programs stack cleanly. Others offset each other.

Concurrent SSDI + SSI

If your SSDI payment is below the SSI federal benefit rate ($967/month in 2026), you may receive a supplemental SSI payment to bring your total up to that level. Your SSDI counts as unearned income against the SSI calculation, with a $20 general exclusion. For example, if your SSDI is $750/month, SSI would add approximately $237/month ($967 - $750 + $20 exclusion). This also gives you both Medicare (from SSDI, after the waiting period) and Medicaid (from SSI, immediately). The SSDI vs. SSI comparison has detailed calculation examples.

VA Disability + SSDI

These two programs do not offset each other. VA disability compensation is not counted as income for SSDI purposes. A veteran can collect a full VA disability payment and a full SSDI payment simultaneously. This is one of the most valuable benefit combinations in the system — and one that many eligible veterans miss.

VA Disability + SSI

VA disability compensation is counted as unearned income for SSI purposes. If your VA payment exceeds the SSI income limit, you won't qualify for SSI. For veterans with low VA ratings, a concurrent SSI claim may still be worthwhile.

State TDI + Federal Programs

State TDI is temporary. It typically can't be collected at the same time as SSDI or SSI (which require long-term conditions). However, someone who starts on state TDI and whose condition worsens into a long-term disability can transition to filing for SSDI or SSI while their state benefits are still active. The state benefit ends when the temporary coverage period expires; the federal benefit, if approved, continues.

The Stacking Rule of Thumb

  • VA + SSDI: Full stack. No reduction.
  • SSDI + SSI: SSI supplements a low SSDI payment up to the federal rate. Not a full double payment.
  • VA + SSI: VA income reduces SSI. Only works at lower VA rating levels.
  • State TDI + SSDI/SSI: Sequential, not concurrent. TDI first for short-term, then transition to federal if condition persists.

How Long Benefits Last

There's no single answer. Duration depends on the program, the nature of your condition, and periodic reviews.

Continuing Disability Reviews (SSDI and SSI)

The SSA schedules periodic reviews to verify you still meet the disability standard. How often depends on the expected trajectory of your condition. "Medical improvement expected" cases get reviewed within 6 to 18 months. "Medical improvement possible" cases are reviewed every 3 years. "Medical improvement not expected" cases — severe, permanent conditions — are reviewed every 5 to 7 years. If a review finds you've medically improved to the point where you can work, benefits stop.

SSDI to Retirement Conversion

SSDI automatically converts to Social Security retirement benefits when you reach full retirement age. The payment amount doesn't change — it just shifts from the disability program to the retirement program. Your Medicare coverage continues uninterrupted.

VA Re-Evaluations

The VA can schedule re-examinations to assess whether your condition has improved. If your rating has been in place for five or more years, the VA faces a higher legal standard to reduce it. After 20 years at the same rating, reductions become extremely rare. Permanent and total (P&T) ratings are generally not subject to future re-evaluation.

State TDI Duration

State programs have hard time limits. California SDI covers up to 52 weeks. New Jersey TDI covers up to 26 weeks. When the coverage period ends, the benefits stop regardless of whether you've recovered. This is why people with conditions that extend beyond the TDI window need to transition to a federal program.

Children and Transitions

Children receiving SSI based on disability are re-evaluated at age 18 using adult criteria, which are different and often more stringent. SSDI auxiliary benefits for a disabled worker's children end at age 18 (or 19 if still in high school) unless the child is themselves disabled, in which case benefits can continue as Childhood Disability Benefits (CDB).

Why People Get Denied

Denial is common. Understanding the real reasons — not the form-letter explanations — makes the difference between a fixable setback and a wasted year.

1. Medical Evidence Is Weak or Incomplete

This is the number-one reason across all programs. "I have a diagnosis" is not enough. Agencies need records showing the severity and functional impact of your condition over time. Gaps in treatment, vague doctor notes, and missing specialist reports sink otherwise valid claims. A file full of "patient reports pain" without objective testing, imaging, or functional assessments gives the reviewer nothing to approve.

2. Earnings Are Too High (SSDI)

If you're earning above the SGA limit ($1,620/month for non-blind individuals in 2026) when you apply, SSDI will deny you at step one regardless of how severe your condition is. The program's logic is straightforward: if you can earn that much, you're not disabled under their definition.

3. Assets or Income Exceed Limits (SSI)

SSI's $2,000 asset cap catches people off guard. A checking account with $2,100 on the day of review is disqualifying. Deemed income from a spouse can push someone over the income limit even if the applicant personally earns nothing. These are non-medical technical denials — your condition may clearly qualify, but the financial test fails.

4. Wrong Program

Filing SSDI without sufficient work credits. Filing SSI with too many assets. Claiming VA disability without being able to establish service connection. Each of these is a structural mismatch between the applicant and the program — fixable, but only if you know where you actually belong.

5. Procedural and Paperwork Errors

Missed deadlines. Incomplete forms. Failure to attend a consultative examination. Not responding to a request for additional information. These administrative failures can result in denial or claim abandonment even when the underlying case is strong. The bureaucracy doesn't chase you — it processes what's in front of it, and moves on.

A Denial Is Not a Final Answer

Across SSDI and SSI, a large percentage of eventually-approved claims were initially denied. The appeals process exists because the system acknowledges its own high error rate at the initial level. If you're denied, the first step is understanding why — then deciding whether to appeal, reapply, or redirect to a different program.

How to Improve Your Odds

This isn't a checklist of generic tips. The single most impactful thing you can do is choose the right program before you file. Everything else follows from that decision.

Get the Program Selection Right

Before you fill out a single form, map your situation to the four program categories above. Check work credits for SSDI. Calculate assets for SSI. Confirm service connection evidence for VA. Verify that your state offers TDI if you have a temporary condition. Filing for the wrong program doesn't just waste time — it delays your entry into the right one.

Build Your Medical Record Before You Apply

The strongest claims are filed after the medical record is already solid. That means consistent treatment (not a single ER visit), specialist evaluations, objective testing (MRIs, blood work, psychological evaluations), and clear documentation of functional limitations. If your records are thin, it is sometimes worth investing a few months in building a stronger medical foundation before submitting the application.

Understand What the Reviewer Needs to See

Every program has specific criteria. SSA reviewers follow a structured evaluation. VA raters look for nexus evidence and severity. State TDI processors verify employment and physician certification. When you understand exactly what each reviewer is checking, you can make sure your file contains it. This isn't about gaming the system — it's about presenting a complete case.

Don't Go It Alone on Complex Claims

For SSDI and SSI appeals, attorneys work on contingency (capped fees, paid only if you win). For VA claims, accredited Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs) provide free representation. Navigating complex benefit systems without experienced help is possible, but statistically results in lower approval rates and longer processing times.

File for Everything You Qualify For

There's no penalty for applying to multiple programs simultaneously, and the applications don't interfere with each other. If you're a veteran with a work history and limited savings, you could reasonably be filing VA disability, SSDI, and SSI at the same time. The worst outcome is a denial from one program that doesn't affect the others.

Common Misunderstandings About Disability Benefits

These misconceptions persist because the system itself never clearly corrects them. Each one costs people time, money, or both.

"Disability is one program"

It's not. It's at minimum four separate systems with different agencies, rules, funding, and criteria. Someone who says "I applied for disability" hasn't communicated anything specific enough to evaluate. SSDI, SSI, VA, and state TDI are as different from each other as car insurance is from health insurance — they share a word, not a structure.

"If I'm sick enough, I automatically qualify"

Medical severity is necessary but not sufficient. SSDI requires work credits. SSI requires financial poverty. VA requires service connection. Having a devastating medical condition doesn't override these structural requirements. You must meet both the medical and non-medical criteria for whichever program you're applying to.

"Everyone gets the same amount"

SSDI payments are individualized based on your earning history. Two people with identical medical conditions can receive wildly different SSDI payments. SSI is closer to a flat rate, but income, living arrangements, and state supplements create significant variation. VA compensation is tied to rating percentage and family composition. There is no standard disability payment.

"SSI and SSDI are basically the same thing"

They share an agency and a medical evaluation process. That's where the similarity ends. Different funding, different eligibility logic, different payment structures, different health insurance, different treatment of income and assets. Confusing them is the single most common source of benefit-selection error. Our SSDI vs. SSI comparison page exists specifically to clear this up.

"State disability programs are available everywhere"

Only six jurisdictions operate TDI programs. If you live in Texas, Florida, Ohio, or any of the other 44 states without one, there is no state-level disability benefit waiting for you. Workers in those states rely entirely on federal programs (SSDI, SSI) or private disability insurance purchased by their employer or themselves.

"Getting denied means I don't qualify"

Initial denial rates for SSDI and SSI are above 60%. The majority of eventual approvals happen on appeal — particularly at the administrative law judge hearing level. A denial means the initial reviewer didn't find sufficient evidence in your file at that point in time. It doesn't mean your case is over.

Key Takeaways

What to Remember

  • Four systems, not one. Federal work-based (SSDI), federal needs-based (SSI), veteran (VA), and state-administered (TDI). Each has its own rules, requirements, and pay structure.
  • Eligibility is program-specific. Work history, income, assets, service connection, and state of residence all determine which programs you can access. Medical evidence alone isn't enough.
  • You can qualify for multiple programs at once. VA + SSDI is a full stack. SSDI + SSI is a supplement. Don't limit yourself to one application if more than one program fits.
  • Program selection is strategy, not luck. The wrong program wastes months. The right combination can mean thousands of additional dollars per year and immediate health coverage.
  • Denials are common and often reversible. Don't treat a denial as a final answer. Understand why it happened, fix what's fixable, and use the appeals process.
  • Medical records are your currency. Across all four systems, the strength of your documentation determines your outcome more than the severity of your condition alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know which disability benefit to apply for?

Start with your situation: If you have 10+ years of work history, check SSDI first. If you have very limited income and assets (under $2,000), look at SSI. If you served in the military and have a condition linked to service, pursue VA disability. If you're in California, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, Hawaii, or Puerto Rico with a temporary condition, start with state TDI. Many people qualify for more than one.

Can I receive disability benefits if I've never worked?

Yes, through SSI. Supplemental Security Income has no work history requirement. You must meet the medical definition of disability and fall below strict income and asset limits. Disabled children can also qualify for SSI based on their parents' financial situation. SSDI, however, requires work credits earned through payroll tax contributions.

How much will I receive in disability benefits?

It depends entirely on the program. SSDI averages about $1,630/month in 2026, based on your earning history. SSI pays up to $967/month for individuals. VA disability ranges from $175/month (10% rating) to over $3,800/month (100% rating). State TDI varies widely by state. There is no single "disability payment" amount.

How long does it take to get approved for disability?

State TDI decisions are fastest: usually 2–4 weeks. VA initial decisions average 4–5 months. SSDI and SSI initial decisions take 3–7 months, and appeals can extend the process to 2+ years. The timeline depends heavily on the program, the complexity of your condition, and whether you need to appeal.

What happens if I get denied for disability benefits?

A denial is not the end. For SSDI and SSI, you can request reconsideration, then a hearing before an administrative law judge, then Appeals Council review. For VA disability, options include Higher-Level Review, supplemental claims with new evidence, or a Board of Veterans' Appeals hearing. Approval rates are significantly higher at the hearing/appeal stages than at the initial level.

Can I work while receiving disability benefits?

It depends on the program. SSDI allows limited work below the SGA threshold ($1,620/month in 2026) and offers trial work periods. SSI reduces payments as earnings increase but doesn't cut them off entirely until you exceed program limits. VA disability has no work restrictions at all — you can earn any amount and still collect. State TDI generally requires that you're not working while claiming.

Do disability benefits come with health insurance?

SSDI qualifies you for Medicare after 24 months of payments. SSI typically qualifies you for Medicaid immediately. VA disability allows enrollment in VA healthcare, with priority and copay waivers scaling with your rating. State TDI does not include health coverage. If you receive concurrent SSDI and SSI, you can have both Medicare and Medicaid.

Is VA disability compensation taxable?

No. VA disability compensation is completely tax-free at all rating levels. SSDI benefits may be partially taxable if your total income exceeds certain thresholds. SSI is never taxable. State TDI may be taxable depending on the state and how premiums were paid.

Can I get both SSDI and VA disability at the same time?

Yes, and they do not reduce each other. VA disability compensation is not counted as income for SSDI purposes. A veteran who qualifies for both receives the full amount of each. This is one of the most advantageous benefit combinations available, and many eligible veterans don't realize they can collect both.

What medical conditions qualify for disability benefits?

There's no fixed list that guarantees approval. SSDI and SSI use a "Blue Book" of listed impairments, but you can also qualify by demonstrating that your condition — even if not specifically listed — prevents you from performing any substantial work. VA disability covers any condition connected to military service. What matters most across all programs isn't the diagnosis itself, but the documented severity and functional impact of the condition.
