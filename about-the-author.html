About the Author

Hi, my name is Paul Paradis.

I built this site for one simple reason: people trying to navigate disability benefits are often overwhelmed, under-informed, and left trying to piece together critical decisions from scattered, unclear sources. That situation can cost people time, money, and in some cases, access to benefits they genuinely need.

This site exists to reduce that confusion.

I’m not a lawyer, and I’m not connected to the Social Security Administration or any government agency. I don’t make decisions on claims, and I don’t offer legal representation. What I do is research, organize, and explain complex processes in a way that’s easier to understand — especially for people who are dealing with stress, health issues, or time pressure.

The focus here is practical clarity. Every page is built to answer real questions people have when they’re in the middle of the process — not just general explanations, but the kinds of details that actually affect what someone does next. That includes things like what documents matter most, when outside help might be useful, where free resources fit in, and what to realistically expect at each stage.

There’s a reason this kind of information matters. When you’re dealing with a disability claim, small misunderstandings can turn into delays, missed opportunities, or unnecessary frustration. A lot of existing information either assumes prior knowledge or skips over the parts people actually struggle with. I wanted to build something that fills those gaps without adding noise.

Everything on this site is written with that in mind. The goal isn’t to impress — it’s to be useful. That means keeping explanations straightforward, avoiding unnecessary jargon, and being honest about what something can and cannot do. If a step is optional, that’s stated. If something is commonly misunderstood, that’s clarified. If a resource helps in some situations but not others, that distinction is made clear.

Another important part of this site is staying neutral. You won’t find promises about outcomes, guarantees of approval, or pressure to take a specific path. Disability claims are individual, and what works for one person may not apply to another. The information here is meant to support better decisions, not replace them.

I also take accuracy and consistency seriously. Pages are structured to stay focused on a single topic so they don’t overlap or repeat each other unnecessarily. When a topic connects to another area — like documents, appeals, or outside help — it’s explained in context rather than blended into unrelated sections. That helps keep things clearer for readers and more reliable overall.

Transparency matters too. This site may include references to third-party resources, but it does not represent or endorse any government agency. Any external links are included because they are relevant to the topic being discussed. The intent is always to point people toward legitimate, useful sources — especially official government pages or established support organizations.

If you’re using this site, the best way to approach it is as a guide — something to help you understand the process, organize your next steps, and avoid common mistakes. It’s not a replacement for professional advice where that’s needed, but it can help you get a clearer picture of where you stand and what to do next.

At the end of the day, this site reflects a simple idea: people deserve clear, honest information when they’re dealing with something as important as a disability claim. That’s what I’ve tried to build here.

— Paul
